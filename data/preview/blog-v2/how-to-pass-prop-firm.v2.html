Part 2 — The 3 Evaluation Models

1. Single-phase evaluation

One sustained period of trading to a profit target. Apex, Bulenox, TakeProfit. Faster but unforgiving — if you blow up, the entire eval restarts.

2. Two-phase evaluation

Phase 1 to target, then Phase 2 with lower target. FTMO, FundedNext. Slower but provides resilience — passing Phase 1 confirms style before deeper commitment.

3. Direct funding (instant)

No evaluation. Pay higher upfront fee, trade immediately. Funded accounts have stricter rules. Suitable for proven traders with capital who want to skip evaluation friction.

For new prop firm traders: single-phase is highest-prior for fast funding. For traders who want to validate strategy: two-phase is more deliberate.

Part 3 — Drawdown Mechanics

The single most important variable. The drawdown rule is the rule that decides if you survive.

Trailing intraday drawdown

The DD line follows the highest UNREALIZED equity reached during any session. Wicks to a high then back lock the DD line higher permanently.
Example: $50K account, $2,500 trailing. Hits $51,500 intraday peak → DD line moves to $49,000. Closes at $50,500. DD line stays at $49,000.
Verdict: hardest for swing traders. Manageable for scalpers who close positions quickly.

Trailing EOD drawdown

The DD line follows only the END-OF-DAY equity. Intraday wicks don't move the DD line.
Same example: peak end-of-day $51,000 → DD line moves to $48,500.
Verdict: much more forgiving. Most swing traders prefer this.

Static drawdown

The DD line is fixed at account-start minus DD amount. Never moves.
Verdict: the easiest mathematically. Rarely available outside FTMO Swing accounts.

For complete deep dive: see Trailing vs EOD Drawdown.

A 6% target sounds modest. The math:

  • 20 trading days, 6% gain = +0.3% per day.
  • With 0.5% risk per trade, 0.3% per day requires roughly 60% win rate on 1R setups, or 50% win rate on 1.2R-average setups.
  • Trivial for proven traders. Hard for beginners.

Part 5 — The Daily Loss Limit

Most firms enforce a daily loss limit (5% on $50K = $2,500). Once breached, the eval restarts.

The trap: after a losing morning, traders revenge-trade to "make it back." They breach the daily limit. The eval ends.

The hard rule

After 2 consecutive losses in any session, stop trading. Do not take a 3rd trade that day. This single rule prevents 90% of daily-limit blow-ups.

Part 6 — Min Trading Days

Most firms require 5-10 minimum trading days before allowing a pass. Why this exists:

  • Prevents gamblers who pass on a single lucky day.
  • Forces traders to demonstrate consistency.
  • Aligns with the firm's funding economics (longer demonstration = better risk assessment).

Strategic implication: build profit slowly across 7-15 trading days rather than racing for a single big day. Race-style passing has dramatically lower success rates.

Part 8 — Consistency Rules

The "30% rule": no single trading day can represent more than 30% of total eval profit. The rule exists to prevent lottery-style passes.

Strategic implication

If you make $1,500 on Day 1 of a $3,000 target, you cannot pass until Day 1 is no longer >30% of total. Either spread the profit across more days or scale back size.

Verifying current consistency rules before starting an eval is critical. Some firms have softened this rule recently.

Part 9 — Top 6 Strategies That Pass Evaluations

1. ES/NQ futures scalp with 1R risk

Best for Apex and trailing intraday firms. Quick in-out trades. Win rate ~58-62% with 1R average win.

2. Swing breakout with multi-day hold

Best for FTMO Swing or Bulenox EOD. Breakouts from daily structure. Win rate ~52% with 2.5R average win.

3. VWAP mean reversion (intraday)

Best for any firm. Fade extreme VWAP deviations. Win rate ~64% with 1R average win.

4. Opening range breakout (first 30 min)

Best for Apex/TopStep. Trade breakout of the first 15-30 min range. Win rate ~54% with 1.5R average.

5. London session FX

For FTMO and forex-friendly firms. EURUSD/GBPUSD during London/NY overlap. Win rate ~58% with 1.2R average.

6. Options premium selling

For firms allowing options (FTMO, FundedNext). 0DTE iron condors or weekly credit spreads. Win rate ~65% with 0.8R average.

Most successful eval traders specialize in 1-2 of these strategies, not all 6.

Part 10 — Pre-Evaluation Checklist

  1. Demo the platform 2 weeks. Same platform, same conditions. Validate strategy.
  2. Document trading plan. Setups, entry rules, stops, targets, sizing — all in writing.
  3. Position size calculator ready. Spreadsheet or app, pre-calculated for the eval account size.
  4. Daily journal template. Track every trade in R units.
  5. Hard rules written. Max consecutive losses, max daily loss, time-of-day restrictions.
  6. Eval simulator practice. Run a mock eval on demo first.
  7. Quiet trading environment. No distractions. Same setup every session.
  8. Reset capital reserved. Budget for 2-3 possible resets if needed.

Part 11 — The First Week Playbook

Days 1-3

Trade smaller size than normal (50% of standard). Validate that your system works under live eval conditions. Goal: stay flat to slightly green.

Days 4-5

If consistently profitable, scale to standard size. Goal: build 20-30% of target profit by end of week 1.

Critical: do NOT try to pass in week 1. Min trading days and consistency rules make it inefficient and risky.

Part 12 — The Middle Phase

Days 6-15. Slow compounding. The trader's enemy here is boredom. Sticking to the plan when nothing exciting is happening is the hardest skill to develop.

Goal: build 50-75% of target by end of week 3. Stay well clear of daily loss limit and trailing drawdown.

Part 13 — The Final Push

Days 16-30. Closing in on target. The temptation: aggressive sizing to finish fast.

The rule

If you're at 80% of target by week 4, scale DOWN size. Do not try to lock it in with one big trade. Continue your normal sizing and let the math complete.

Most eval blow-ups happen in the final 20% of the path to target. The trader gets impatient. Reverts to bad habits. Blows the entire eval.

Part 14 — After Funding

Passing the eval is the easy part. Keeping the funded account is the real test.

The first payout

Most firms split 50/50 on first payout. Profit threshold varies. Apex requires $200+ minimum withdraw; FTMO has different thresholds by account size.

The 90-day cliff

Median funded account survival: 90 days. The pattern of failure:

  1. Passed eval with conservative strategy.
  2. After funding, switched to more aggressive style (felt confident).
  3. Took larger position sizes than during eval.
  4. One bad week breached drawdown.
  5. Account terminated.

The rule

Trade exactly the same strategy that passed the eval. Do not change anything for at least 90 days post-funding. If the conservative strategy worked to pass, it will work to compound. Confidence-driven style changes are the leading cause of funded-account death.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which firm is easiest to pass?

Depends on style. Scalpers: Apex. Swing traders: Bulenox or FTMO Swing. Match style to drawdown structure.

Multiple firm evaluations at once?

Technically yes. Practically discouraged. Splitting focus reduces success rate.

Cheap reset trick worth it?

If you blew on a single bad day with otherwise solid trading: yes. If strategy doesn't have edge: fix strategy first.

Signals or bots allowed?

Bots: usually restricted. Signals: allowed but discouraged. Discretionary skill must be demonstrated.

Scalpers or swing traders favored?

Firm rules vary. Match your style to the firm's mathematical structure.

How to avoid the daily loss limit?

Stop trading after 2 consecutive losses. Daily limit is a wall, not a target.

Typical payout %?

Most firms: 50/50 split for first payouts. Some increase to 80-90% trader after multiple successful payouts.

Multiple funded accounts allowed?

Yes. Apex up to 20. Most firms 3-5+.

News trading really banned?

Apex: no restriction. FTMO/TopStep: restricted within minutes of high-impact news. Check current rules.

Typical funded account lifespan?

Median 90 days. 1 year: ~15%. 2 years: ~5%.

Banned for rule arbitrage?

Yes. Hedging across accounts, copy-trading: grounds for ban. Trade clean.

Blew the eval — retry?

Single bad day: reset and retry. No strategy edge: don't retry until demo proves edge.

Conclusion

Passing a prop firm evaluation requires three things: a verifiable trading edge, disciplined execution, and the right match between your style and the firm's mathematical structure.

Most retail traders skip the third. They sign up with Apex because they saw a YouTube ad, never realizing that Apex's trailing intraday drawdown is structurally hostile to swing traders. Pick the firm whose math matches your style.

Do that, demo the platform, write your plan, follow your hard rules, build slowly across the eval window, and continue exactly the same strategy after funding. The 2% survivors do these six things consistently.
