Part 1 — Sizing Is the Most Important Trading Skill

This claim is true even though every popular trading education contradicts it. The contradictions exist because sizing is boring and entries are exciting. Excitement sells books. Boring math grows accounts.

Three quantitative points:

  1. A strategy with 60% win rate and 1.5R average win produces +0.6R expectancy per trade. With 1% sizing, that's +0.6% per trade. With 0.5% sizing, +0.3% per trade. With 2% sizing, +1.2% per trade.
  2. The same strategy with the same expectancy will produce wildly different equity curves at different sizing levels.
  3. Position sizing is the only variable in this calculation that the trader fully controls.
You cannot control your win rate. You cannot control your average R. You can fully control your sizing. The variable you control is the variable that compounds.

Part 2 — The 4 Sizing Methodologies

1. Fixed dollar

Risk the same dollar amount on every trade. Beginner-friendly. Doesn't scale with account growth.

2. Fixed fractional (the 1% rule)

Risk a fixed percentage of current account balance. Scales naturally with growth and contraction. The standard.

3. Volatility-based (ATR sizing)

Risk a fixed percentage but compute stop distance based on Average True Range (ATR). Position size adjusts automatically to market volatility.

4. Kelly fractional

Mathematical optimal sizing based on win rate and win/loss ratio. Theoretical foundation. Use 1/4 Kelly in practice to handle estimation error.

Part 3 — Fixed Fractional

The foundation of professional position sizing.

position_size = (account × risk_pct) / (entry − stop)

Equities example

Account: $10,000
Risk: 1% = $100
Entry: $50
Stop: $48
Stop distance: $2
Position size: $100 / $2 = 50 shares

Futures example (ES)

Account: $50,000
Risk: 1% = $500
Entry: ES 5,500
Stop: ES 5,490 (10 points)
$ per ES point: $50
Position size: $500 / (10 × $50) = 1 contract

Forex example (EURUSD)

Account: $25,000
Risk: 1% = $250
Entry: 1.0900
Stop: 1.0880 (20 pips)
$ per pip per standard lot: $10
Position size: $250 / (20 × $10) = 1.25 standard lots (round to 1 or use mini lots)

Part 4 — Volatility-Based Sizing

Fixed fractional with stop distance computed via Average True Range (ATR).

Method: ATR(14) is the 14-period average of (high − low + max(0, prior close − low) + max(0, high − prior close)). Take this value, multiply by a constant (typically 1.5-3x), and that's your stop distance.

Example

SPX in low-vol regime: ATR(14) = 30 points. Stop = 2 × ATR = 60 points.
SPX in high-vol regime: ATR(14) = 80 points. Stop = 2 × ATR = 160 points.

With the same 1% risk budget, your position size shrinks in high-vol and grows in low-vol. Total risk stays constant. The market's volatility absorbs the variance.

Volatility-based sizing is the standard for systematic traders. Most discretionary traders adopt it after 1-2 years of fixed fractional discipline.

Part 5 — Kelly Criterion

The mathematical optimum.

f = (bp − q) / b

where:
p = probability of winning
q = probability of losing = 1 - p
b = ratio of win to loss

Worked example

Win rate p = 0.55, b = 1.5 (win 1.5R, lose 1R)
q = 0.45
f = (1.5 × 0.55 - 0.45) / 1.5 = (0.825 - 0.45) / 1.5 = 0.25 = 25%

Full Kelly says risk 25% per trade. Don't. Use 1/4 Kelly = 6.25%. Still aggressive for retail. Most professionals stay at 1-2%.

Why 1/4 Kelly

Full Kelly assumes your win rate and win/loss ratio estimates are perfect. They are not. Your real edge is always less than your sample suggests. 1/4 Kelly accounts for estimation error and keeps drawdowns manageable.

Part 6 — The Scaling Pyramid

Adding to winning positions in a controlled, predefined sequence.

The 3-tier scaling pyramid

  1. Initial entry: 1R risk at original setup.
  2. Add #1: At +1R unrealized profit, add 0.5R of new risk. Raise initial stop to break-even.
  3. Add #2: At +2R unrealized profit, add 0.25R of new risk. Raise both stops to lock in profit.

Total cumulative position size: 1.75x initial. Total cumulative risk: 0.75R or less (because earlier adds have raised stops). The pyramid asymmetrically allocates more capital to confirmed winners.

Why this works

Trend-following strategies have a fat right tail — the big winners are much bigger than the average. Pyramiding into the right tail captures more of it.

The opposite of pyramiding (adding to losers, "averaging down") destroys accounts. Same mechanic in reverse.

Part 7 — The Distribution Pyramid

Taking profits in stages instead of all at once.

The 3-stage exit

  1. 1/3 of position at 1R profit. Stop on remainder moves to break-even.
  2. 1/3 at 2R profit. Stop on remainder moves to +1R.
  3. 1/3 with trailing stop or structural target.

Math

If all three legs hit targets: total = (1/3 × 1R) + (1/3 × 2R) + (1/3 × 4R typical for runner) = 0.33 + 0.67 + 1.33 = +2.33R average per win.
With 50% win rate and -1R losses: expectancy = (0.5 × 2.33) - (0.5 × 1) = 0.67R per trade. Excellent.

Comparison to all-out exits

If you exit the entire position at the first target (1R), your win average is 1R. Expectancy = (0.5 × 1) - (0.5 × 1) = 0R. Break-even.
The distribution pyramid converts a break-even strategy into a profitable one purely through exit mechanics.

Part 9 — Correlation Adjustment

Two correlated positions are effectively one larger position. Sizing must account for this.

Correlation matrix (approximate)

PairCorrelation
SPX vs NQ0.85+
SPX vs Russell0.75
SPX vs DJIA0.95+
SPX vs EURUSD-0.2 to +0.3 (regime-dependent)
Gold vs Bitcoin0.3-0.5
SPX vs Treasury yields0.3-0.5 inverse

Rule of thumb

Total correlated long exposure (sum of equity positions × correlation factor) should not exceed 2-3% of capital. If you're long ES at 1% risk and add long NQ at 1% risk, you have approximately 1.8% effective correlated risk (because correlation is 0.85). Adding a third correlated long pushes you near the limit.

This is what destroyed Long-Term Capital Management in 1998. Their positions were technically uncorrelated under normal regimes but became 1.0 correlated during the Russian default. Always model worst-case correlation, not average.

Part 10 — Prop Firm Sizing

Tighter than personal accounts. Why:

  • Trailing or strict DD rules.
  • Limited time to recover from losses.
  • Termination on rule violation.
  • Daily loss limits.

Recommended sizing

  • Apex/TopStep (trailing intraday): 0.25% per trade pre-profit-lock, 0.5% post-lock.
  • Bulenox/TakeProfit (trailing EOD): 0.4% per trade pre-lock, 0.7% post-lock.
  • FTMO Swing (static): 0.5-1% per trade. Closer to personal account sizing.

Conservative sizing produces lower PnL but dramatically higher survival. The prop firm pays 50-90% of profits — survival is the multiplier.

Part 11 — Operational Calculator

Build a simple spreadsheet:

InputCell
Account sizeA1
Risk %B1
Entry priceC1
Stop priceD1
$ per pointE1
Position size=(A1*B1)/(ABS(C1-D1)*E1)

For every trade, fill in the inputs. Output is your position size. Mechanical. Repeatable. Removes emotion from sizing entirely.

Most platforms have built-in calculators. The spreadsheet exists for traders who want to verify and adjust independently.

Part 12 — Top 8 Sizing Mistakes

  1. Sizing by feel. "This setup feels strong, let me put on more." Never works long-term.
  2. Larger size after wins. Overconfidence bias. Sizing should be proportional to account, not to recent results.
  3. Smaller size after losses. Timidity. Misses the compound recovery.
  4. Not adjusting for volatility regime. Same position size in low-vol and high-vol means different actual risk.
  5. Ignoring correlation. Two long SPX + two long NQ = one big long, not four separate trades.
  6. Same size across instruments. ES is not GBPUSD is not BTC. Each has different volatility characteristics.
  7. No max position cap. Even with proper formula, single positions over 5% of capital create concentration risk.
  8. Sizing into bad setups same as good ones. Best setups deserve full size. Marginal setups deserve half size or skip.

Part 14 — Building a Sizing System

Step 1 — Choose methodology

Start fixed fractional. Move to volatility-based after 6 months. Add correlation adjustment when running multiple positions.

Step 2 — Pre-trade calculation

Every trade: open the calculator. Plug in account, risk %, entry, stop. Output is your size. Use the size. Do not adjust.

Step 3 — Track per setup

R-multiple journal per setup. Identify which setups deserve full size and which deserve half size.

Step 4 — Adjust quarterly

Every quarter: recompute account-based risk amounts. As account grows, dollars per trade grow proportionally.

Step 5 — Handle drawdowns

During -10% drawdown: cut risk in half (0.5% instead of 1%) until you recover above original equity. This is "anti-Martingale" sizing and protects against catastrophic losses.

Step 6 — Scale after milestones

After verified +50% account growth with positive expectancy across 100+ trades: increase risk from 1% to 1.25%. Small steps, never large jumps.

Frequently Asked Questions

Simplest sizing method to start?

Fixed fractional with 1% rule. Mechanical, repeatable, mathematically sound.

How to size futures contracts?

position = risk_$ / (stop_points × $_per_point). Round down to whole contracts or use micros.

Is Kelly really useful?

Full Kelly is too aggressive. 1/4 Kelly is reasonable for proven traders. Most professionals stay below quarter Kelly anyway.

How to handle correlated positions?

Treat as one larger position. Cap total correlated risk at 2-3% of capital.

Should sizing change with volatility?

Yes. Volatility-based sizing automatically adjusts via ATR.

Size up after winning streak?

With a scaling plan, not emotionally. Equity grew so 1% is now more dollars (same ratio).

Right size for $1K account?

$10 per trade. Micros and fractionals. Focus on building skill.

Prop firm sizing different?

Yes. 0.25-0.5% on prop firm vs 1% personal. Stricter DD rules.

Scaling rules affect sizing?

When prop firm scales account, recompute size for new account size.

Pyramiding into winners really better?

For trend-following: yes. Captures more of the right-tail winners.

Options sizing differ from futures?

Yes. Size by premium paid (defined risk) or by max loss (credit spreads).

Best calculator app?

Most platforms have built-in. A simple spreadsheet with the formula is more reliable than any app.

Conclusion

Position sizing is the only edge that compounds across all strategy regime changes. It works in bull markets and bear markets. It works in low volatility and high volatility. It works on stocks, futures, forex, and crypto. It's the boring superpower.

Master fixed fractional. Move to volatility-based. Add correlation adjustment. Pyramid into winners with discipline. Distribute exits in stages. Adjust risk per account stage. Tighten for prop firm accounts.

Do these seven things mechanically for twelve months and your equity curve will look like the ones professional traders post — slow grinding compound growth with shallow drawdowns. Not exciting. Just inevitable.
