Part 1 — The Hierarchy of Trading Skills

Trading skills form a pyramid. From bottom to top:

  1. Risk management (the foundation that holds everything else up)
  2. Trade management (managing positions while active)
  3. Setup selection (which patterns/setups to trade)
  4. Market view (top-down macro/structural perspective)

Most retail traders build the pyramid upside down. They start with market views ("the Fed will cut rates so I'll buy tech"), apply random setups, manage trades by feel, and treat risk management as an afterthought.

The result is mathematically guaranteed loss. You cannot compensate for poor risk management with better predictions. Mathematically, you can compensate for poor predictions with great risk management.

The market does not care how right you are. It pays you for surviving long enough to be right about something. Risk management is what keeps you alive long enough for your edge to express itself.

Part 2 — What Is 1R

1R = the dollar amount you risk on a single trade. It's the fundamental unit of trading currency.

Example: You have a $50,000 account. You risk 1% per trade. 1R = $500.

Every trade is then measured in R-multiples, not dollars:

  • Win of $1,000 = +2R
  • Win of $500 = +1R
  • Loss of $250 = -0.5R
  • Loss of $500 = -1R
  • Loss of $1,000 (you let it run) = -2R (mistake)

Why R-multiples matter: they standardize performance across instruments, account sizes, and timeframes. A trader who averages +0.4R per trade is profitable regardless of whether they trade $1K or $1M accounts. The mathematics scale.

Part 3 — The 1% Rule

Risk no more than 1% of capital per trade. This is the canonical retail rule. Why 1%?

The math of recovery

DrawdownGain required to recover
10%11%
20%25%
30%43%
50%100%
75%300%
90%900%

A 50% drawdown requires a 100% gain just to break even. A 90% drawdown requires a 900% gain. These math facts are why position sizing dominates everything else.

Why 0.5% is often better

For new traders, 0.5% per trade is mathematically safer. A 20-loss streak at 0.5% = -10% account. At 1% = -20%. At 2% = -40%. New traders underestimate their drawdown probability.

When 2%+ becomes acceptable

Only with verified positive expectancy, multi-year track record, and disciplined execution. Even then, most professionals stay at 1% because the marginal compound return from 1% to 2% does not justify the risk.

Part 4 — Position Sizing Formula

The mechanical core of risk management.

position_size = (account × risk_pct) / (entry_price − stop_price)

Worked example — Equities

Account: $50,000
Risk %: 1% = $500
Entry: $100
Stop: $98
Stop distance: $2
Position size: $500 / $2 = 250 shares

Worked example — Futures (ES)

Account: $50,000
Risk: $500
Entry: ES 5,500
Stop: ES 5,495 (5 points stop)
$ per point on ES: $50
Position size: $500 / (5 × $50) = 2 contracts

Worked example — Forex

Account: $50,000
Risk: $500
Entry: EURUSD 1.0900
Stop: EURUSD 1.0880 (20 pips)
$ per pip per standard lot: $10
Position size: $500 / (20 × $10) = 2.5 standard lots (round to 2)

Part 5 — The 4 Stop-Loss Methodologies

1. Hard stop (limit order in market)

Pros: Mechanical. No discretion required. Protects against fast moves.
Cons: Can be hit by wicks. Visible in the order book.

2. Mental stop (price level, no order)

Pros: Hidden from market. Flexible.
Cons: Requires discipline. Fails when emotion overrides plan. Catastrophic on gaps.

3. Technical stop (structure-based)

Stop just beyond significant support/resistance, swing high/low, or pattern invalidation. The logic: if price reaches that level, the trade thesis is wrong.

4. Volatility stop (ATR-based)

Stop distance = N × ATR(14). Adjusts to market conditions. Wider stop in volatile regimes; tighter in calm regimes.

Most professional retail traders use a hybrid: technical stop with volatility adjustment, placed as a hard stop order.

Part 6 — R-Multiple Trade Analysis

The most underused performance tool in retail trading.

Log every trade in R units instead of dollars. After 50 trades, calculate:

  • Win rate (% of trades closed positive)
  • Average winning trade in R
  • Average losing trade in R
  • Cumulative R
  • Max R drawdown

Patterns emerge quickly:

  • Setup A: 60% win rate, avg +1.2R, avg -1.0R = positive expectancy
  • Setup B: 45% win rate, avg +2.5R, avg -1.0R = highly positive expectancy
  • Setup C: 70% win rate, avg +0.8R, avg -1.5R = negative expectancy

You would intuitively keep Setup C (high win rate) and drop Setup B (low win rate). The math says drop Setup C and double down on Setup B. R-multiple analysis catches this inversion immediately.

Part 7 — Expectancy

Expectancy = (win_rate × avg_win_R) − (loss_rate × avg_loss_R)

Example: 50% win rate, average win +2R, average loss -1R.
Expectancy = (0.5 × 2) - (0.5 × 1) = 1.0 - 0.5 = +0.5R per trade.

This means: over hundreds of trades, you expect +0.5R per trade on average. With 1% risk per trade, that's +0.5% per trade. 100 trades = +50% expected return.

Why win rate is overrated

A 30% win rate strategy can be highly profitable if the average win is 5R and the average loss is 1R.
Expectancy = (0.3 × 5) - (0.7 × 1) = 1.5 - 0.7 = +0.8R per trade.

A 70% win rate strategy can be unprofitable if the average win is 0.5R and the average loss is 2R.
Expectancy = (0.7 × 0.5) - (0.3 × 2) = 0.35 - 0.6 = -0.25R per trade. Negative.

The retail obsession with win rate is one of the most expensive cognitive biases in trading.

Part 8 — Drawdown

Drawdown is the largest peak-to-valley decline in account equity. It is the single most important metric for evaluating a strategy.

Acceptable drawdowns

  • Personal account, new trader: 10-15% max. Beyond this, most quit.
  • Personal account, proven trader: 20-30%. Still psychologically painful.
  • Hedge fund: 10-20% max before LP redemptions trigger.
  • Prop firm rules: 4-10% (varies by firm).

Recovery time

Time to recover from drawdown matters more than the drawdown itself. A 20% drawdown that recovers in 3 months is different from a 20% drawdown that takes 18 months. The latter destroys psychological capital.

Part 9 — Kelly Criterion

The mathematical optimal bet size for maximizing log-utility.

f = (bp − q) / b
where p = win probability, q = loss probability (1-p), b = win/loss ratio

Example

p = 0.6, q = 0.4, b = 2 (win 2R, lose 1R)
f = (2 × 0.6 - 0.4) / 2 = (1.2 - 0.4) / 2 = 0.4 = 40%

Full Kelly says risk 40% per trade. Don't.

Why 1/4 Kelly in practice

Full Kelly assumes your win rate and win/loss ratio estimates are exact. They never are. With estimation error, full Kelly produces enormous drawdowns and frequent ruin.

Half Kelly: still very aggressive. 1/4 Kelly: pragmatic compromise between mathematical optimum and real-world variance tolerance.

1/4 Kelly of the example above = 10%. Still aggressive for retail. Most professionals risk 1-2% per trade — well below quarter Kelly.

Part 11 — Psychological Capital

The hidden risk most frameworks ignore. Confidence is a finite resource.

Loss aversion math (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979): losses hurt 2× more than equivalent gains feel good. A -3R losing day creates 2× the psychological damage of a +3R winning day.

Tilt — the degraded decision-making state after losses — is one of the most expensive failures in trading. A trader on tilt makes worse decisions, takes worse trades, and compounds losses rapidly.

The 3-loss rule

After 3 consecutive losses in a day: stop trading. Walk away. Review tomorrow. This single rule has saved more accounts than any technical innovation in trading history.

Part 12 — Top 10 Risk Management Mistakes

  1. No stop loss
  2. Moving stops further away when price moves against position
  3. Averaging into losing positions ("hoping for reversal")
  4. Position sizing by feel rather than formula
  5. Risking more after losses (revenge trading)
  6. Risking less after wins (timidity that misses compound growth)
  7. Ignoring correlation across positions
  8. Not tracking R-multiples
  9. No max daily loss limit
  10. No max consecutive loss rule

Each of these is responsible for a large fraction of blown accounts. Eliminate all ten and survival rate triples.

Part 13 — Case Studies

Case 1 — Renaissance Medallion (1988-present)

The most successful fund in history averaged ~66% annual returns over 30+ years. Their drawdowns rarely exceeded 5%. The strategy is opaque, but their risk management is publicly documented: small position sizing, rapid trading, mathematical discipline.

Case 2 — Long-Term Capital Management (1998)

Two Nobel laureates. Ten PhDs. The most sophisticated risk model on Wall Street. Blew up because they ignored correlation in their risk calculations. The 1998 Russian default created correlations they had not modeled, and their leveraged positions all moved together. $4.6 billion of equity vanished in five weeks.

Lesson: model assumptions matter. Correlation kills.

Case 3 — Trader X, 2020 COVID crash

Anonymous retail trader profiled in Jack Schwager's series. Had a $250K account at start of February 2020. COVID hit. Most retail traders blew up. Trader X: stopped trading on Feb 25 after a single 2% loss day, did not trade again until March 30, lost only 4% total, and entered the recovery rally with full capital. Discipline saved more than skill.

Part 14 — The Operational Risk Playbook

Pre-trade checklist

  1. What setup is this? (Named, in my system.)
  2. Where is my stop? (Specific price, structural justification.)
  3. What's my position size? (Calculated mechanically.)
  4. What's my target? (Specific R-multiple or structural level.)
  5. What's my max risk? (Should equal 1R.)
  6. Does this trade fit my correlation budget? (No more than 2-3% total correlated risk.)

Daily rules

  • Max risk per trade: 1% (or 0.5% if newer trader, 0.25% if prop firm).
  • Max trades per day: typically 3-5 for swing, 5-10 for intraday.
  • Max consecutive losses: 3 → stop trading.
  • Max daily loss: 3-5% → stop trading.

Weekly rules

  • Max weekly drawdown: 5-8% → review strategy.
  • Mandatory journal review.
  • Mandatory R-multiple tally.

Monthly rules

  • Calculate expectancy.
  • Drop setups with negative expectancy (sample size 30+).
  • Scale up R if account compounded.

Frequently Asked Questions

What % should I really risk per trade?

0.5-1% for new traders. 1-2% for proven traders. Never more than 2%. Prop firm: 0.25-0.5%.

How do I calculate position size on futures?

position = risk_$ / (stop_points × $_per_point). Example: $500 risk, 20pt stop on ES = $500 / (20 × $50) = 0.5 contracts. Round down.

Hard stop or mental stop?

Hard for retail. Mental only for experienced traders with verified discipline. Never mental on overnight options/futures.

What if my stop gets hit by a wick?

Use wider stops based on ATR or structure. Do not move stops further during the trade.

Averaging down ever ok?

Planned scaling at predefined levels: yes. Random averaging into losing trades: never.

Max drawdown to accept?

Personal: 15-25%. Prop firm: pick a firm matching your tolerance (Apex 4-7% trailing, Bulenox 6-8% EOD, FTMO 10% static).

How do prop firms calculate drawdown?

Trailing intraday, trailing EOD, or static. See dedicated article on this topic.

Can I scale risk after wins?

Yes — with a scaling plan. Wrong: emotional sizing-up. Right: equity grew so 1% is now more dollars (same ratio).

How does correlation affect total risk?

Two SPX + two NQ + one Russell = one big long. Cap total correlated exposure at 2-3%.

Good win rate to aim for?

35%+ is profitable with proper R. Win rate matters less than average R. 35% win × 3R win beats 65% win × 1R win.

Is 1% rule outdated for small accounts?

No. 1% of $5K is $50. Mathematically correct. The alternative guarantees ruin.

Handle a 5-loss streak?

Stop at 3 losses. If continued: stop for week. Review.

Conclusion

Risk management is the most boring topic in trading and the only one that compounds. Master the 1R doctrine. Calculate position size mechanically. Place stops structurally. Track R-multiples religiously. Compute expectancy monthly. Enforce hard daily limits.

Do those six things consistently for twelve months and your survival probability triples. Survival is the prerequisite for everything else.
