The VIX missed it. Gamma Exposure didn't. Between January 2018 and October 2023, every single one-day SPX move above 3% happened during a negative-GEX regime — periods when option dealers were structurally forced to amplify price movement instead of dampening it. If you're trading the S&P 500, prop futures, or any liquid index, and you're still using VIX as your only volatility gauge, you're flying with one eye closed.

This is what we're going to break down: what Gamma Exposure (GEX) is, how the math works without the academic veil, why dealer hedging mechanically drives intraday tape, and how to read the GEX regime before you take a position. By the end, you'll know why "low VIX" and "low volatility" stopped being synonyms after 2020 — and what to watch instead.

The problem VIX silently created

VIX is implied volatility derived from a strip of SPX option prices, interpolated across two near-term contracts. It's a forecast. The CBOE itself puts the correlation between VIX and the next 30-day realized volatility at 0.75. Not bad — until you notice that VIX correlates 0.85 with the realized volatility of the previous month. The implied is more accurately describing the past than the future it claims to predict.

Worse, VIX loses resolution at the low end. SqueezeMetrics ran the numbers: when VIX closes in its lowest quartile, the next day's SPX standard deviation is 0.51%. In its second-lowest quartile, it's 0.66%. A VIX of 12 means roughly the same as a VIX of 15 when you're trying to forecast tomorrow's range. For a futures trader sizing positions around expected daily ATR, that's a 30% miss in expected range — disguised as precision.

This isn't a flaw in the VIX calculation. It's a flaw in the question. VIX asks "what does the option market expect?" GEX asks something different: "what are option dealers structurally forced to do?"

What dealer hedging actually does to price

Every retail option trade has a counterparty on the other side. Roughly 90% of US listed options flow through dealers — market makers whose business is to buy at the bid, sell at the ask, and never hold directional risk overnight. To stay neutral, they hedge in the underlying.

Concrete example, straight from the SqueezeMetrics paper:

  • A dealer sells you a single 20-delta put. To neutralize the convexity, the dealer shorts ~20 shares of the underlying.
  • Price drops. The put delta rises to 50. The dealer must short an additional 30 shares to stay neutral — selling into weakness.
  • Price rises instead. The put delta falls to 0. The dealer buys back the short — buying into strength.

This isn't speculation. It's mechanical, mandatory, and continuous. Multiply by thousands of strikes and millions of contracts, and you get a deterministic re-hedging flow that can damp volatility or amplify it depending on the net position the dealer book is sitting on. That net position has a name. That's GEX.

The two regimes (and why this is binary)

GEX is computed strike-by-strike, then summed. The formula isn't the point — the sign is.

Positive GEX means dealers are net long gamma. As price rises, their effective deltas rise too, so they sell into strength. As price falls, deltas fall, so they buy into weakness. This is a mean-reverting force. It tightens daily ranges, suppresses realized vol, and creates the kind of slow grind higher that the 2017 and 2024 tapes are infamous for.

Negative GEX is the inverse and the mirror. Dealers net short gamma must sell into weakness and buy into strength to stay delta-neutral. Every move down forces more selling. Every move up forces more buying. This is the structural fuel under flash crashes, vol expansion, and the kind of one-day 3%+ moves that the VIX never saw coming.

The SqueezeMetrics data is unambiguous on the magnitude. The standard deviation of next-day SPX returns by GEX quartile:

  • Highest GEX quartile (most positive): 0.55%
  • Second-highest: 0.85%

That's a 55% jump in expected range as you move one quartile down the GEX curve. The VIX equivalent comparison (0.51% → 0.66%) is a 29% jump. GEX has nearly twice the resolution at the low-vol tail — exactly where prop traders get blown up by surprise expansions.

The math, finally

For a single strike, the contribution to GEX is:

GEX (calls) = γ × OI × 100
GEX (puts)  = γ × OI × (-100)

Where γ (gamma) is the rate of change of the option's delta per 1-point move in the underlying, OI is open interest at that strike, and 100 is the standard multiplier from contracts to shares.

The sign flip on puts isn't a typo. Under the standard assumption that protective puts are bought by investors and sold by dealers (vs. calls, which are typically written by investors and bought by dealers as part of covered-call and collar structures), the dealer's gamma exposure to puts is structurally short. Sum across every strike and every expiry to get the index-level GEX figure.

For SPX specifically, GEX is denominated in dollars rather than shares — SPX options have no underlying stock, only futures and ETF proxies. The conversion changes the units, not the logic.

0DTE: the part of this story that's only five years old

Until 2022, daily expiries on SPX didn't exist as a major flow. By late 2023, 0DTE (zero days to expiration) options accounted for over 40% of all SPX options volume on some days. This isn't a fringe development. It reshaped the dealer book.

The Volland whitepaper from Ad Deum Funds (December 2023) is one of the few peer-reviewed attempts to map the 0DTE-specific dealer dynamics. The key finding: on a 0DTE basis, dealers are more sensitive to changes in implied volatility than to changes in the underlying. And the dominant greek at the 0DTE horizon stops being gamma — it becomes charm.

Charm is the rate of change of delta over time. As an expiry approaches, every in-the-money option's delta converges to ±1.00 and every out-of-the-money option's delta converges to 0. This convergence accelerates exponentially in the final hours of the trading session. A dealer who was perfectly hedged at 10:00 AM is, by 3:00 PM, holding a delta that has drifted simply because the clock ticked — without the underlying moving at all.

The practical implication: the last 60 minutes of US cash session now carry a structural hedging flow that wasn't there pre-2022. If you're trading futures into the close, your edge or your blow-up is more often than not a function of 0DTE charm-driven re-hedging, not "the news."

How a trader actually uses this

Most retail GEX content stops at "positive good, negative bad." That's roughly true but operationally useless. Three rules that hold up under backtest:

1. Match strategy to regime, not the other way around. Mean-reversion strategies (fade extremes, range scalping, premium selling) work in positive-GEX environments because the dealer flow agrees with you. Breakout strategies (momentum continuation, directional swing) work in negative-GEX environments because the dealer flow becomes a tailwind. Running the wrong strategy in the wrong regime isn't bad luck — it's structural mismatch.

2. The flip zone is where positions die. The GEX level where the dealer book crosses from positive to negative is roughly observable from public data (SpotGamma, Tier1Alpha, MenthorQ all publish it daily). Within ~50 points of that flip on SPX, hedging flow becomes erratic — the cleanest "no trade" zone you'll find. Sizing in there is sizing into uncertainty about who's hedging which direction.

3. Large gamma walls are real support and resistance — until they're not. A strike with extreme positive gamma exposure (e.g. 5,500 calls on SPX) tends to act as a magnet on expiration days. Price pins to it. But if the underlying breaks through a major gamma wall, the dealer hedging on the other side accelerates the move instead of damping it. Treat gamma walls as targets, not unconditional stops.

Honest caveats — because the model has them

The SqueezeMetrics framework rests on four assumptions, and all four are approximations:

  1. Every option trade is facilitated by a delta-hedging dealer. Not literally true — some institutional flow is uncrossed — but close enough on SPX-scale liquidity.
  2. Calls are sold by investors, bought by dealers. Empirically defensible because of the dominance of covered-call writing, but it weakens for single-name equities where speculative call buying is heavy.
  3. Puts are bought by investors, sold by dealers. Holds for index puts (used as protection); weaker for single-name names with put-skewed retail speculation.
  4. Dealers hedge precisely to the option delta. In reality, they use hedging bands to balance cost against risk — the actual hedge is lumpier than the model assumes.

The Volland paper is essentially an attempt to relax assumption 4 and add charm/vanna sensitivity that SqueezeMetrics treats as second-order. If you're using free GEX data (SpotGamma, MenthorQ), you're getting the SqueezeMetrics-style model. Good enough for most traders. If you want the granular dealer book — that's a paid product, and worth it only if you trade size into expiry.

The bottom line

VIX tells you what option prices imply about future variance. GEX tells you what option positioning mechanically forces dealers to do. In a market where 90% of option flow is dealer-facilitated and 40% of SPX option volume happens on its expiration day, the second number does a better job of explaining the tape.

You don't need to compute GEX yourself. Free dashboards publish it daily. What you do need is to stop interpreting price action without checking the regime first. The same setup behaves differently in positive-GEX (mean-reverting, range-bound) and negative-GEX (trending, gap-prone) conditions. If you're trading prop futures and your win rate dropped from 55% to 38% in March 2020, that wasn't an alpha problem. That was a regime change you didn't see.

The S&P doesn't move because of headlines. It moves because dealers are forced to hedge, and the direction of that forced flow is a function of what investors bought and sold last week. Read the book. Read the regime. Then trade.
