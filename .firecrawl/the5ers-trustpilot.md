The “3% daily loss limit” is NOT simply based on your balance, and it does NOT only apply to closed losses. It is calculated from your equity at the start of the day (including floating profit), which creates a major hidden risk.

What they don’t make clear enough is this:

If you start the day in profit, that profit becomes part of your allowed loss buffer. If the market pulls back and your floating profit decreases — even if you never closed the trade — that drop is counted as a loss toward your daily limit.

For example:

If you start the day at $10,500 equity (including profit), your loss limit is based on that amount. If your trade retraces and your equity drops to $10,200, that $300 reduction is counted as a loss — even though you are still in overall profit.

This means:

\- You can violate the daily loss rule without ever closing a losing trade

\- You are penalized for normal market pullbacks

\- Holding trades or letting them “breathe” becomes extremely risky

This rule heavily disadvantages traders who:

\- Hold positions overnight

\- Trade higher timeframes

\- Allow natural retracements

It effectively forces you to close trades early or micromanage positions to avoid breaching the rule.

In my opinion, this is not trader-friendly risk management — it is a restrictive rule that protects the firm while putting traders at a disadvantage.
